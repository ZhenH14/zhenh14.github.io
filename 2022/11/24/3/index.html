Our daily dose of Marxist education as Chinese

HUANG Zhen Lv1
  2022-11-24 01:18 2022-11-24 01:18 Created   2023-08-21 21:52:25 2023-08-21 21:52 Updated  
  • Random Stuff|乱七八糟 
 1.2k Words  5 Mins

This is not some new stuff. Just me connecting the dots I’ve acquired from different periods.

Until recently, after diving into Dr. Fuchs’ books on Marxism, I’ve come to realize how many Chinese people, including myself, have this almost naive confidence in being able to spot Marxist thinking in high school or university education. As Chinese students, we often complain about how ethics classes and political courses in college are just droning on, reciting the text word for word – and they actually do, no doubt about that. Back when I was in undergrad, the teachers understood it well and some even turned it into horror storytime. Some folks who’ve read Marx’s original works scratch their heads: why not just teach the original stuff as it is, instead of twisting it?

The answer? I’m not quite sure. But reading Dr. Fuchs’ books, I’ve been surprised to find out I’m more “Marxist-ified” than I ever thought.

For instance, when it comes to history, if someone praises a historical figure’s impact, we’d say, “Their role wasn’t that crucial; if it wasn’t them, someone else like John or Jane would’ve done it.” I tend to think the same way when reading history books. This amplification of historical trends has its merit, but as I realized, it’s just one of many theories, and people who enjoy pondering might grow wary of its seemingly self-evident logic. This theory is known as historical determinism.

Similarly, when discussing socioeconomics, if someone emphasizes the superiority of a system, others might refute it with the Marxist principle, “economic base determines superstructure.” The idea that material well-being drives intellectual pursuits seems unassailable. It aligns with ancient Chinese sayings like “a full stomach leads to indulgence,” or even Maslow’s hierarchy of needs. Though not a theory per se, it’s a popular logic that’s accepted as an indisputable fact – even though many countries showcase completely opposing trajectory.

And then there’s the emphasis on how changes in production modes reshape political patterns, so everything is deduced through the lens of production modes, like Marx’s “Asiatic mode of production” from his “Critique of Political Economy.”

Chinese folks don’t necessarily learn these from strictly Marxist political courses, but more so from history and Chinese textbooks. In history textbooks, they don’t label historical determinism as a certain perspective; they present it as a tool to sum up all historical issues. Thus, even someone like me, who’s not inclined towards that narrative or educational approach, struggles to realize they’re adopting Marxist viewpoints. Guess that’s part of why I chose to study social sciences.

一直到最近我阅读了Dr. Fuchs的有关马克思主义的书后，我才发现很多中国人（也包括我）对于在高中或大学教育中是否能够识别马克思主义思想的灌输有着近乎天真的自信。作为中国学生，我们总是说，思想品德课和大学的政治课是总是无聊地照本宣科——而它们事实上也的确如此，我上本科的时候，老师深谙其中缘由，甚至干脆将课堂发展成恐怖故事会。还有一些阅读过马克思原著的人会不解：目前教育系统下所教授的是如此无趣，当阅读原著时，才发现马克思主义并非如此，为何不干脆原原本本去教授呢？

我不知道这个问题的答案。但是在阅读Dr. Fuchs的书的过程中，我惊讶的发现我比自己想象中的更加马克思主义化。

例如，一谈到历史，如果看到有人称赞某位历史人物的作用，他们便会讲“此人的作用没有那么大，如果那时候不是他，那么也会有张三，李四去做这件事。”当我在阅读历史书的时候，我也总是倾向于这样思考。这种放大历史趋势的思维固有其道理，但如果你发现它们只是无数理论中的一种时，爱思考的人下次再这样想时，可能会害怕这种逻辑萌生的理所当然感。这种理论叫做历史决定论。

再例如，当提到社会经济的时候，如果有人强调制度优越性，那么人们就倾向于以“经济基础决定上层建筑”进行驳斥。吃饱穿暖，身上有余钱才会考虑精神享受，这似乎是一个无懈可击的逻辑，它还能与中国古话的“饱暖思淫欲”又或马斯洛需求层次呼应。虽然这算不上理论，但是这种逻辑却十分流行，以至于人们笃定认为这是无可争议的事实——尽管有许多国家却展现了完全相反的逻辑。

又比如，强调生产方式改变了政治格局，因此一切逻辑以生产方式进行演绎，这是由马克思在《政治经济学批判》中提出的“亚细亚生产方式”。

中国人并不从全是马克思主义的政治课上学到这些，而是从历史和语文教科书。在历史教科书上，他们并不告诉你历史决定论是某个观点，而是将其作为总结一切历史问题的工具。因此，即使是像我这般，完全不倾向这种叙事和教育模式的人，也很难意识到自己正在接受马克思的观点。我猜这是我为什么决定学习社会科学的原因之一。
